Media & Influencer Coverage - Typhoon Karding

ABS-CBN coverage and influencer mentions

GMA coverage and influencer mentions

Chart Description:

The data from both ABS-CBN and GMA during Typhoon Karding underscores the critical role of media coverage on social media in shaping public awareness, emotional response, and real-time action during natural disasters. The contrast in engagement levels between the two networks—ABS-CBN's tweets averaging hundreds to thousands of reposts and likes, versus GMA’s typically lower double-digit figures—reveals how content framing, timing, and platform resonance impact audience behavior.

ABS-CBN’s tweets, particularly those providing urgent warnings—such as signal advisories and expected impact zones—and emotional appeals like prayers or community aid, saw substantial amplification through reposts and high like counts. For instance, one tweet warning about “catastrophic effects” received nearly 3,000 likes and 1,388 reposts, indicating a strong public impulse to share actionable and emotional content. These high-engagement posts demonstrate that effective disaster communication on social media must be immediate, emotionally resonant, and easily shareable.

Meanwhile, GMA’s content—although similarly informative—generally garnered less engagement. Their updates focused more on formal announcements and institutional efforts, which, while important, appeared less emotionally engaging or urgent in tone. Posts highlighting government coordination or late-stage weather developments attracted minimal interaction. This difference suggests that audiences on platforms like Twitter are more responsive to content that feels directly relevant to personal safety or community welfare, rather than institutional updates.

From a broader perspective, this analysis highlights the vital importance of media organizations on social media as both information disseminators and emotional anchors during crises. High engagement can help ensure that critical updates reach wider audiences quickly, enabling faster preparation, response, and even recovery. Social media dynamics—especially the speed and scale of user amplification—can turn a single tweet into a life-saving alert or a rallying point for aid. In sum, the effectiveness of media coverage in disasters is deeply tied to how well it leverages social media's strengths: timeliness, emotional engagement, clarity, and shareability. The ABS-CBN and GMA data not only reflect differing levels of engagement but also reveal how media organizations must continuously adapt their digital strategies to ensure that life-saving information truly reaches—and moves—the public.

ABS-CBN Engagement Data Analysis

Based on the numbers in the dataset, the engagement metrics—reposts, likes, and replies—reveal how users responded to different types of tweets during Super Typhoon Karding. The tweet with the highest engagement overall is Tweet 2, which warned of the typhoon’s catastrophic effects and called for immediate preparation. It received 1,388 reposts and 2,990 likes, indicating that users saw it as highly urgent and important to share. Similarly, Tweet 0, a prayer post, had 731 reposts and 2,651 likes, showing strong emotional resonance and a shared sense of hope or solidarity. Tweets that reported significant updates, such as signal number changes (Tweets 6 and 5), also had high reposts (493 and 272 respectively) and hundreds to over a thousand likes, reflecting the audience’s desire to spread critical information. Replies, though generally lower, spiked in Tweet 8 with 84 replies, where the focus shifted to the lack of post-typhoon aid in an affected community. This suggests that users were more likely to respond or engage in discussion when a tweet addressed a perceived injustice or unmet need. Other tweets had minimal replies, ranging from 2 to 21, suggesting that news-style bulletins are typically not conversational but rather informative. Overall, the data shows that the public is most responsive—in both amplification and emotional engagement—to tweets that are either highly urgent (disaster warnings) or deeply resonant (prayers, calls for help), with the numbers reflecting distinct user behaviors depending on the content’s nature.

GMA Engagement Data Analysis

The GMA Network dataset reflects a significantly lower level of engagement compared to the ABS-CBN dataset, based on the numerical data for reposts, likes, and replies. The most engaged tweet in this set—Tweet 2, reporting that President Marcos Jr. was in contact with Cabinet officials—garnered only 56 reposts, 174 likes, and 11 replies, suggesting moderate public interest in leadership updates. Tweets invoking prayer or offering emotional appeals (like Tweet 1) had slightly better performance with 23 reposts and 90 likes, indicating that faith-based messaging had some traction, though still limited. In general, most tweets across the dataset received fewer than 25 reposts, under 100 likes, and 1 or no replies, with several tweets (e.g., Tweets 8–13) having virtually no engagement at all. Tweets about weather updates post-landfall, which typically included technical details, were the least engaged, often receiving 0–1 reposts or likes. This suggests a decline in public interest once the peak of the typhoon had passed or that these updates lacked urgency or emotional appeal. Overall, the data shows that while GMA disseminated timely and relevant updates, the posts were less amplified and interacted with by users. This could reflect differences in audience behavior, post content style, or platform strategy. Numerically, the lower engagement suggests that users were either less compelled to share or emotionally invest in GMA’s posts or were more focused on other sources, such as ABS-CBN, for information and expression during Typhoon Karding.

Typhoon Henry Data Description:

Based on the collected data from both the community and the government, we can observe notable differences in their social media activity and engagement during Typhoon Henry (#HenryPH). The community produced a total of five posts, while the government, represented by PAGASA-DOST, released only three posts. While the graph focuses solely on the total number of posts, the broader analysis considers the likes, reposts (retweets), and replies to better understand user engagement. The community's posts generated a total of 2,138 likes, 847 retweets, and 25 replies. In contrast, government posts garnered 1,152 likes, 548 retweets, and a higher number of 59 replies. This suggests that while the community generated more content and received higher engagement through likes and retweets, the government’s posts prompted more direct interaction, as reflected in the number of replies. These replies likely came from users seeking clarification or further updates, which is typical when dealing with official announcements during emergencies. Community users such as Ariel Rojas and The Varsitarian contributed significantly to the high engagement levels, using their platforms to inform, clarify misinformation, and provide real-time updates from local areas. These posts were often more relatable and emotionally resonant, which may explain the surge in likes and shares. On the other hand, government tweets were formal, direct, and informational—serving as trusted bulletins rather than conversation starters. This dynamic illustrates a common pattern in social media during crises: government agencies act as authoritative sources, delivering verified and timely information, while community members function as amplifiers, commentators, and localized informants. Together, they form a collaborative information ecosystem. The likes and retweets reflect content that resonates emotionally or socially with users, while replies highlight users’ need for clarity, reassurance, or more detailed answers. From an academic perspective, especially in courses related to media, communication, or data science, this comparison highlights the evolving role of social media in public discourse and crisis communication. It underscores the importance of analyzing both quantitative metrics (like post count and engagement rates) and qualitative dynamics (such as content type and tone) to understand how information flows and influences public behavior during critical events.

This section provides a rich dataset for SMD by illustrating how social media facilitates collective action and community mobilization during disasters. The distinct characteristics of community posts (grassroots, urgent, personal appeals) versus government posts (official, resource-focused, broader announcements) highlight different communication strategies and network behaviors. The community's focus on immediate, localized needs demonstrates the power of social media in enabling rapid, decentralized responses. The observation that government posts have higher engagement despite the community's direct action focus is significant for SMD; it may point to the established reach and network centrality of official accounts, or a public tendency to engage with authoritative information sources even while participating in grassroots efforts. This data is relevant for understanding the symbiotic relationship between formal and informal response networks, the flow of information and resources between them, and how social media platforms serve as a crucial interface for coordinating diverse efforts in disaster management.

GMA & ABS-CBN Data Comparison:

Based on the compiled data regarding social media activity related to Typhoon Henry (#HenryPH), we can observe how GMA Integrated News and ABS-CBN News used their platforms and how the public engaged with their content. The focus of the chart for these two media outlets is on the total number of likes, reposts, and comments (replies) received across their posts. GMA Integrated News posted a total of seven tweets related to Typhoon Henry. These posts collectively garnered 48 likes, 19 reposts, and 6 replies. While the volume of GMA's tweets was relatively high, the engagement per post was modest. Most of GMA’s content focused on weather updates, hazard warnings, and forecasts, which are highly informative and helpful for public safety but tend to elicit less emotional or viral responses. Their highest-performing tweet in terms of likes received 19 likes, and the most retweeted post had 10 reposts. These figures suggest that while GMA maintained consistent coverage and aimed to inform their audience, the posts did not generate widespread interaction or viral traction. In contrast, ABS-CBN News posted only three tweets, but the engagement levels were significantly higher. These three tweets accumulated a total of 3,540 likes, 1,022 reposts, and 66 replies. One tweet in particular — a video showing the impact of Typhoon Henry on a concert venue — received 2,300 likes, 341 reposts, and 31 replies, highlighting the strong emotional and visual impact of the content. This demonstrates how visual and human-interest content can dramatically increase engagement, even if fewer posts are made. Comparing both media outlets, we see that GMA had more frequent updates, likely aiming for consistent and real-time weather monitoring, while ABS-CBN leveraged fewer but more engaging content, capturing moments that resonated more with their audience. From an academic or media studies perspective, this comparison illustrates how content type and delivery style affect engagement metrics, even when covering the same event. It also reflects the importance of storytelling and visual elements in increasing interaction, especially in a fast-paced social media landscape where attention is fleeting and competition for views is high.

This comparison is highly relevant to Social Media Dynamics as it illustrates differing patterns of information diffusion and network influence during a crisis. This highlights the power of decentralized networks and non-traditional influencers (like an entertainment entity) in crisis communication. The data underscores how perceived utility and shareability of content can drive virality, bypassing official channels. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for SMD researchers and practitioners aiming to optimize crisis response by leveraging both authoritative sources and the organic reach of community-driven content.
